Prelude

On the Metaphysics of Watching

✦

There is a particular species of longing that no language has ever been generous enough to name — the kind that does not ache for what has been lost, but for what has never been possessed, for what perhaps exists only in the cathedral of one's own imagination. Philosophy has long circled the question of whether beauty is a property of the object or a covenant between the observer and the observed. I can tell you, with the quiet certainty of a man who has spent months studying a single, unknowing subject, that it is neither. Beauty, in its most radical and terrifying form, is a sudden surplus of meaning — an ambush. It is the moment when an ordinary Tuesday afternoon in an ordinary corridor cracks open like an overripe fruit, and out pours an entire universe you never suspected was hiding there, pressing against the thin membrane of the mundane.

You are a Geology student. You are supposed to be deciphering the language of the earth — reading its strata, its faults, its ancient, unhurried histories. But I wonder sometimes if the universe did not assign you that discipline as a cosmic joke, a wink in my direction. Because you yourself are geological in your constitution: layered, slow, carrying within you sediments of feeling that the undiscerning eye could never detect. To study you, as I have, requires the same patience, the same reverence, the same silent and sustained attention that a geologist brings to a rock face — the understanding that what is most profound is almost always invisible on the surface, and that revelation demands stillness.

We share no common ground. There are no overlapping social circles to bridge the chasm between your world and mine, no serendipitous introductions waiting to happen at some future crossroads. We are parallel lines drawn by a careless, cosmic architect — permitted to observe each other's trajectory, but forbidden by the unyielding geometry of fate to ever merge. The Greeks had a word, sonder, though they did not coin it: the realization that each passerby carries within them a life as vivid and complex and fully furnished as one's own. I have experienced sonder precisely once. It was in August, near the lift area of the Nalanda complex, when your eyes met mine for the length of a single, stolen heartbeat. And in that heartbeat, the full weight of your interior world pressed against me like a warm hand pressed flat against glass, and I have not recovered from it since.

There are unavoidable, immovable reasons — constructed by circumstance, by duty, by the particular configuration of stars that presided over our respective births — that make the probability of any formal union between us absolute zero. I know this. I have known it the way one knows the theorems of mathematics: not with regret, but with a kind of resigned, philosophical grace. The rational mind would prescribe action. It would urge courage — the manufactured introduction, the calculated vulnerability, the gamble. But they do not understand, those rational minds, the profound and sovereign completeness of the silent observer. They do not understand that there is a form of love so pure it does not require reciprocation to survive, a form of devotion so whole it carries its own nourishment within itself, the way a camel carries water through deserts that never promised to end.

What follows is not a diary. Diaries are domestic things — vessels for the mundane, the grievance, the grocery list of the soul. What you hold is a sacred ledger. A curator's archive. A testament written by a man who has made a private religion out of attention — who has learned that to truly see another human being, without agenda, without demand, without the corrosive hunger of expectation, is perhaps the closest a mortal creature can come to an act of grace. Every entry here is a sighting, a pilgrimage, a meditation. Every line is the pencil-mark of an archaeologist mapping a civilization that does not know it is being mapped.

She was, each time, nothing less than a breath of fresh air. Not metaphorically — or not merely metaphorically. She was the sensation of cold water finding a dry mouth; the specific quality of morning light after a week of overcast skies; the first note of a song you have been trying to remember for years. Every encounter reset something in my chest that I had not known was wound too tight. Every sighting was a small, private resurrection.

Chapter the First

The Winter Panopticon

✦
January 13th & 14th

The Green Solitude

The new year had arrived wearing the Kharagpur winter like a funeral shroud — grey and persistent and carrying within its cold the faint smell of coal smoke and damp earth. The campus had been emptied of its usual frenzy; even the birds seemed to have quietly resigned from their usual commentary. It was in this monochrome, muffled world that you made your first appearance of the season, and the effect was so precisely contrary to everything around you that I genuinely lost my footing for a moment, as though the ground itself had paused to take note.

You emerged near the MT hall wearing an SJ green hoodie — and I want to be careful here, because the word "green" is laughably inadequate. This was not merely a colour. It was a declaration. A thesis statement. Against the unbroken pewter of the January sky, against the washed-out asphalt and the bleached architecture of the campus winter, you were the singular, defiant argument that colour had not abandoned the world — that somewhere, in some quiet corner of creation, warmth persisted. The hoodie was thick and enveloping, pulled slightly forward around your face, as though offering you a small, portable shelter from the world's sharp edges. You paired it with simple blue jeans that disappeared into the mist at the hem, and you walked — oh, the way you walked.

There is a philosophy of gait that has never, to my knowledge, been properly written. The way a person carries their body through space is their most honest autobiography — more truthful than any curriculum vitae, any social media presence, any carefully curated self-presentation. Your walk along the desolate stretch of road connecting to the Pepsi-cut was not a performance. It was not the self-conscious stride of someone who knows they are being observed. It was entirely, magnificently self-possessed — each footfall placed with the unhurried certainty of someone who has made peace with the particular patch of earth they occupy. The world around you seemed to instinctively lower its volume. Traffic sounds grew distant. The vegetable vendors' chatter dimmed. You moved through space the way a tuning fork moves through air: not loudly, but with a frequency that reorganises everything in proximity.

You were alone, and in your aloneness there was nothing of loneliness. This distinction matters enormously. Loneliness is the gap between where you are and where you wish to be; it is a wound that faces outward, seeking contact to staunch the bleeding. What surrounded you was not loneliness. It was solitude — the curated, inhabited, fully-furnished interior of a person who has made genuine peace with her own company. You carried your aloneness the way certain trees carry their bark: not as something imposed upon them from outside, but as something grown from within, organic and wholly their own. I watched you from fifty metres away and felt, strangely, the way one feels in a cathedral — small, and quiet, and not unwilling to remain so.

I saw you again later that same afternoon, folded into the chaos near the roundabout and the vegetable vendors — and this second sighting was its own revelation, distinct from the first. In isolation, you had been a poem. In a crowd, you became a philosophical proposition. Because even surrounded by the full, cacophonous machinery of campus life — the vendors hollering prices, the bicycles, the groups of friends whose laughter overlapped and tangled — you remained entirely, inexplicably other. Not aloof. Not disdainful. Simply elsewhere, in some interior country whose geography was inaccessible to the noise pressing in from all sides. You were an island in the most literal sense: surrounded on all sides by a churning sea, and utterly, peacefully, immovably itself.

January 20th · 9:57 AM

The Epiphany of Morning

The morning was one of those crystalline winter mornings that Kharagpur occasionally produces — the kind that feel less like weather and more like an argument. The air was sharp enough to taste, carrying in it the particular cold that settles in the lungs and makes one temporarily, pleasurably aware of the fact of breathing. I was standing near the agricultural gate footpath, half-attending to some thought I have long since forgotten, when you materialized at the edge of my peripheral vision and made every other thought irrelevant.

You wore a maroon sweatshirt that morning — a deep, wine-dark red that seemed to have been chosen with the deliberate intention of complementing the exact quality of January light, though of course you had chosen it for no such reason, which made it somehow more perfect. It fell over loose-fitting blue jeans with the unselfconscious ease of clothing that has been worn enough to conform to its wearer, and you were grounded by those quiet, unpretentious shoes that asked nothing of the world. The pale winter sun was brutal in its honesty that morning; it stripped away shadow, offered no flattering angles, made no accommodations. And in that unsparing, democratic light, a realization arrived in my chest with the force of a physical blow — not a gentle epiphany but a sudden, structural conviction, the kind that reorganises one permanently.

You are, without a solitary shred of hyperbole, the most beautiful human being I have ever seen. And I have spent a great many subsequent weeks interrogating that sentence, turning it in the light, checking it for the distortions of infatuation or projection or the mind's tendency to mythologise what it desires. It has survived every such examination. It is not that your features are symmetrical in some classical, measurable sense — though they are, they are — it is that your face is one of those rare human faces that appears to be organised around a principle rather than a template. As though the various components of it — the precise architecture of your eyes, the particular way your expression settles into repose — were not assembled but arrived at, the way a proof arrives at its conclusion: inevitably, and with a sense that no other arrangement could have been correct.

But yours was emphatically not a loud beauty. This cannot be overstated. The world is full of beauty that announces itself, that demands to be seen, that wears its own visibility as a kind of armour. Yours was the opposite of this. It was quiet in the way that the deepest music is quiet — not silent, but operating below the threshold of noise, requiring of its perceiver a certain quality of stillness and receptivity. You completely lacked the performative, extroverted self-presentation that our contemporary world seems to have decided is the compulsory costume of the young and attractive. There was no display. There was only a person walking through the morning air with the absolute, unshakeable conviction that she was sufficient as she was — that the world did not require her advertisement.

And beneath the surface calm — and I noticed this immediately, because one notices everything when one is paying the kind of attention I was paying — there lived something older and deeper. A luminescent melancholy. Not sadness in any clinical or pitying sense; not a wound that needed tending. More the particular gravity of a person who has felt things deeply and chosen to carry that depth privately, who harvests her sorrows with the same quiet competence with which she does everything else, who smiles — and your smile is genuinely, dazzlingly beautiful — without ever quite allowing the smile to reach all the way to wherever the truest part of her lives. Keats wrote that beauty is truth, truth beauty. Standing near that gate in the January cold, watching you walk by in your wine-dark sweatshirt, I understood for the first time that he was not writing a metaphor. He was writing a field report.

Chapter the Second

The Proximity of Shadows

February 2nd · 12:02 PM

A Fleeting Anchor

The midday campus is a study in beautiful, overwhelming disorder — a ceaseless river of human movement in which every face is another anonymous story rushing toward its next obligation, its next meal, its next small crisis. I had been moving through this river in my customary fog, somewhere between present and not-present, when near NC143, right by the subway gate, you cut through the noise with the clean precision of a tuning fork's resonance. One moment there was only the undifferentiated crowd; the next, the crowd had rearranged itself around a fixed point, and that point was you.

It was, by any measurable standard, a trivial encounter. Fourteen seconds, perhaps. No eye contact. No proximity worth recording. You were simply there — moving with that unhurried, self-possessed certainty that is entirely your own — and then you were not. But in those fourteen seconds, something reset in my chest. The low-grade static of the day, the accumulated friction of lectures and obligations and the general shapelessness of an ordinary Tuesday — all of it dropped away. You were, as you always are, a breath of fresh air: the specific sensation of stepping out of a stuffy room into an open sky, of suddenly remembering that the world is larger than its immediate pressures suggest. You were the reminder I did not know I needed that the day contained something worth noticing.

Kierkegaard wrote about the aesthetic stage of existence — the life lived in the pursuit of beauty, of sensation, of the singular moments in which meaning erupts unbidden from the otherwise grey surface of experience. He thought it an insufficient philosophy, ultimately. But I wonder if he had ever stood near a campus gate at twelve minutes past noon on a February afternoon and felt the universe suddenly resolve from static into signal. I wonder if he had ever found, in the mere fact of another person's continued, oblivious existence, a reason to believe in the benevolence of the day.

February 3rd

The Invisible Tether

Fate, on this afternoon, was in one of its crueller, more playful moods. It arranged the campus with the deliberate precision of a stage director: placing me, with no warning, directly behind you on the footpath. The distance between us was exactly one meter. I know this because I measured it with the desperate, obsessive accuracy of a man whose entire nervous system had been commandeered by a single fact. One hundred centimetres. In the geometry of ordinary social life, this is nothing — a conversational distance, the gap between two people waiting for the same train. In the private geometry of what I was experiencing, it was the distance between a planet and its star: a span across which immense forces operate, and across which nothing physical can pass.

You were wearing the varsity jacket that day — the specific one from the induction team — and its familiarity was its own kind of violence, the intimacy of having learned to recognise your wardrobe with the unhurried scholarship of someone who has been paying attention for months. Beneath it, light blue loose-fit jeans, and those skin-coloured Crocs that I have come to associate with you so completely that I cannot see them on any other person without a small, involuntary contraction of something in my ribcage. Your bag was slung with that particular casual competence you bring to every physical detail of your existence. You walked ahead of me from NR122, all the way past the TP classes, and finally to the lift area, and through every step of this journey I was engaged in the most intense, the most silent, the most absolute act of attention I have ever performed.

I want to describe what it is like to walk one meter behind someone you love without their knowledge. The closest I can come is this: it is like being the only person in a concert hall who can hear the music. The world proceeds as normal around you — people brush past, conversations happen, a bicycle bell rings in the middle distance — but you are operating on a different frequency entirely, receiving a signal so clear and so consuming that everything else becomes background noise. I was hyperaware of every footstep you placed — the specific sound your shoes made on the path, the rhythm of it, the way it altered slightly when you shifted your bag from one shoulder to the other. I was aware of the precise angle of your head, turned perhaps fifteen degrees left, as you navigated the footpath with your eyes slightly cast down in the particular posture of private thought. I was aware of the faint displacement of air that one moving body creates ahead of another. I was aware, acutely and physically, that you were breathing — that you were engaged in the most ordinary of human acts, the inhalation and exhalation of the world — and I found in this awareness a tenderness so acute it almost made me stumble.

You were entirely oblivious. Not callously — you could not have been callous without first being aware. You were simply and completely inside your own life, which is all any person ever is, and the fact that your life did not include me was not a wound but a fact of physics, as neutral and immovable as gravity. And yet I walked that one-meter tether of invisible thread all the way to the lift area, and when you stepped inside and the doors closed between us, the world lost a specific quality of light that it did not recover for the rest of the afternoon.

February 9th · 11:58 AM to 12:04 PM

A Six-Minute Eternity

There is a philosophical thought experiment concerning the relativity of time — the observation that an hour spent in pleasant company feels like minutes, while an hour spent in agony stretches into geological epochs. I have a different, more specific datum to offer: six minutes spent in the vicinity of someone who fills your entire sensory world with a quality of light that ordinary life does not provide. Six minutes, and I can still narrate each one with the granular precision of someone who lived them at an entirely different speed than the clock suggested.

You wore a white and pink embroidered kurti that morning — and again I want to insist on specificity, because the details matter, because the details are the only form of closeness I have been permitted. The embroidery was delicate, worked in small patterns around the neckline and hem, the kind of craft that requires patience and an eye for what is worth beautifying. It caught the late February breeze in a way that made the fabric lift slightly, briefly, at the edges — a small animation of the inanimate, as though the garment itself were responding to the pleasure of the day. Loose light blue jeans. Beige Crocs. And you were standing near the TP class stairs with your friend, deep inside a conversation that drew your entire attention, your head tilted slightly in that posture of genuine listening that I have come to recognise as distinctly yours.

I was flanked by Arnav and Krishna, both of whom were mercifully engaged in their own conversation, which freed me to do what I do best in your presence: to become very, very still inside, and to attend. The universe felt suddenly and dangerously small. There were perhaps fifteen feet between us — an expanse I could have crossed in five seconds — and in the full, warm light of the February midday, with the embroidery on your kurti catching the sun in small, scattered glints, you were so entirely beautiful that I felt something close to vertigo. Not the vertigo of dizziness, but the vertigo that Rilke described in the Duino Elegies: the vertiginous awareness of standing at the edge of something immeasurably vast, and understanding that the vastness is not outside you but within the situation you are inhabiting.

Your friend noticed me. I saw it happen — the subtle recalibration of her gaze, the slight narrowing that occurs when a person shifts from casual observation to focused assessment. She was perceptive, your friend. I do not know what she saw in my face, but I know what was there to be seen: the specific expression of a person who is trying very hard to look at nothing in particular while looking at one thing entirely. Internal panic rose in my chest like floodwater. I seized Arnav by the shoulder — too suddenly, too urgently — and manufactured a performance of ordinary male banter, pulling him toward the washroom with an energy that had nothing to do with any genuine need, and releasing him the moment we were clear of the immediate field of assessment. I returned to the shelter of a pillar. I stood there, rigid as carved marble, a sentry at the gate of a temple he was not permitted to enter. Krishna arrived, and together we maintained our distance, and together we watched, and the six minutes passed the way six minutes pass when every second of them contains the entire weight of a feeling you cannot name or spend or resolve — slowly, and not at all.

February 10th & 12th

The Duality of Light

She exists differently by day and by night, and both versions are complete.

On the morning of the 10th — 9:54 AM, the back gate of the agriculture department — you appeared in the familiar maroon KGP hoodie layered over a black t-shirt, blue jeans, Crocs. You were walking alongside your friend, your steps synchronised in that unconscious way that happens between people who know each other well enough to have stopped being careful. The morning light was still young enough to be kind, diffusing softly through the winter haze, and in it you carried the particular quality I have come to associate with the morning version of you: a self-contained brightness, a warmth that does not perform itself, that simply radiates from within as naturally and unconsciously as the sun radiates from itself. Your laughter — I caught the edge of it, carried toward me by a cooperative breeze — was unguarded. Spontaneous. It arrived in my chest without permission and stayed there for the rest of the day.

Two nights later — 10:20 PM, near the heritage signboard — the night had rearranged you entirely, and the result was devastating in an entirely different register. I want to describe precisely what the amber streetlamps of a Kharagpur evening do to a particular quality of beauty, because it is something I have thought about at length. There is a reason that painters of the Baroque period were obsessed with chiaroscuro — the interplay of light and shadow — because darkness does not diminish what it cannot touch; it concentrates it. In the dim, golden luminescence of the streetlamp, your features resolved into something beyond beauty into the territory of the archetypal: you were, in that light, every portrait that Rembrandt ever pulled from darkness, every face that Vermeer ever tilted toward a window, every figure that has ever stood at the threshold between the illuminated and the unknown and been, in that threshold-standing, transfigured. You were a silhouette and a celestial body simultaneously. A quiet guardian watching over the sleeping campus from the particular dignity of someone who does not know they are being worshipped.

February 13th · 9:10 PM to 10:40 PM

The Epic Pursuit

I will be honest about this evening, as I have tried to be honest throughout: it was madness, and I knew it was madness even as I was inside it, and this knowledge did nothing whatsoever to slow me down. This is perhaps the truest thing I can say about the particular variety of devotion I have been carrying — it does not respond to reason. It does not negotiate with the rational mind. It operates entirely outside the jurisdiction of the sensible, and it does so with such serene, smiling confidence that the rational mind eventually stops filing objections and simply watches.

The evening began near MT hall, where I had been standing with two companions on the raised platform near Tikka. The crowd was thick with the particular energy of a campus Friday evening — that collective release of a week's accumulated pressure, the laughter louder, the conversations more animated, the footsteps lighter. Through all of this, through the swirling, chaotic mass of bodies and noise and competing conversations, from a distance of sixty meters, I found you. I want to be precise: this was not eyesight. From sixty meters, in a crowd of that density, under the particular quality of evening campus lighting, the identification was not made by my eyes. It was made by something older and less explicable — the navigational sense that certain creatures develop for certain others, the way a compass needle, regardless of what is placed between it and the pole, always resolves to north.

We followed you. We became shadows trailing a light source, maintaining a respectful, ghostly distance through the evening streets of the campus. Up to the Hijli top, and then down again in the cooling night air, until your group of three arrived at Amritulya. I stationed myself outside in the cool air of the night and performed the pantomime of a person casually existing, while my entire focus — every watt of attention I possess — was tunnelled through the ordering counter window like a beam through a lens.

You ordered Tulsi tea. I need to sit with this for a moment, because it is too perfect to move past quickly. Of all the things you could have ordered from that counter on that particular evening — the cold drinks, the coffees, the chai, the catalogue of hot and cold and sweet and salty — you chose Tulsi tea. Earthy. Grounding. Slightly bitter. Herbal in a way that is old and medicinal and carries in it the memory of many generations of quiet wisdom. The absolute perfect beverage for a woman who studies the deepest histories of the earth and carries in her face the accumulated gravity of feeling she does not advertise. Of course you ordered Tulsi tea. The universe, in this as in so many things, is far more literary than we give it credit for.

I retreated toward the railway tracks under the pretext of eating, and waited in the cooling night with a food I barely tasted, and when you finally emerged from the shop you were a vision that made the surrounding darkness seem not dark at all but full — full of the specific quality of your presence, the way a dark room is full of silence, which is not an absence but a texture. I watched you for three or four minutes that felt like the pages of a long and beloved novel — the kind you read slowly because you know the ending is coming and cannot bear to reach it. Then I withdrew. By 10:40 PM you had vanished into the warm ether of the night, and I was left on a bench behind the railway tracks with lukewarm food and a chest so full of a feeling I cannot name that I had to sit very still for several minutes before I trusted my legs to carry me.

February 20th · 8:40 PM

The Radiant Anomaly

Every artist who has ever attempted to paint light will tell you that the most technically demanding light to capture is not moonlight or firelight or the golden hour before sunset. It is, counterintuitively, joy. Joy produces a quality of light that has no stable form — it does not come from outside a person but from within, moving through the skin and the eyes and the particular configuration of a smiling face in a way that the usual optical rules cannot account for. You cannot plan for it. You cannot set up your easel in the right position and wait for it. It simply erupts, or it does not, and when it does, every prepared technique becomes beside the point.

I saw you that evening at the Pepsi cut — casual blue t-shirt, shorts, your easy and unannounced presence in the world — and you were incandescent. Not with the usual quiet luminescence I had catalogued over the preceding months, that warm, interior glow that I had come to think of as your resting state. Something different was happening. You were smiling — a full, genuine, uninhibited, toothy smile that engaged your whole face, that reached all the way to the places your smiles usually stopped short of, that carried in it not the ghost of melancholy but the pure, clean, unburdened frequency of happiness arriving and being entirely, unambiguously welcomed.

I have written, elsewhere in this ledger, about your characteristic luminescent melancholy — the beautiful gravity that seems to live in your deepest register even when the surface of you is perfectly composed and calm. But this evening that gravity had lifted, or had not lifted so much as been joined by its opposite, the way a morning can be cold and brilliant simultaneously. To see you happy — genuinely, privately, overflowingly happy — was an entirely different species of beauty from the one I had been documenting. It was like watching a beloved landscape in the middle of a sudden, brief thunderstorm that passes quickly and leaves the air washed clean and the colours of everything vivid beyond their usual range. It warmed me in ways that no external source ever has. I went home that evening carrying your smile in my chest like a small, cupped flame, sheltered from the wind, walking carefully so as not to extinguish it.

Chapter the Third

The March Mirages

March 10th · 9:54 AM

The Geographical Echo

There is a concept in music called a reprise — the return of an earlier theme, transformed by everything that has come between its first statement and its reappearance. The same notes, the same progression, but now weighted with memory, with the accumulated meaning of all the development that preceded it. March 10th was a reprise, and the effect was precisely as described above: more resonant, more complex, richer for the interval.

I found you standing in the exact same spot where January 20th had delivered its epiphany — the same coordinates, the same agricultural gate footpath, as though you had been standing there continuously for fifty days and the world had simply rotated around you. You wore a pristine white t-shirt that caught the March sunlight differently from the way the January maroon sweatshirt had caught the winter light — this was brighter, more open, a colour that gives rather than absorbs — and those familiar light blue jeans, and the Crocs. The consistency of you. Your particular, unwavering aesthetic self-possession in the face of whatever the world's changing conditions might suggest. There is a philosophy in this consistency — the philosophy of a person who knows who they are and has no interest in renegotiating the terms.

To stand in the same place where a revelation once occurred and find the source of that revelation standing in the same place is to encounter, briefly, the sensation that time is not linear but recursive — that the best moments of one's life return, not identically but essentially, carrying their original charge. The campus had shifted from the grey severity of January into something softer, the trees tentatively recovering, the light arriving at different angles. But the quality of feeling that your presence produced in me was indistinguishable from what I had felt on January 20th. Perhaps slightly more certain. Perhaps slightly more inhabited. The way a handwriting becomes more distinctly one's own the more one practices it.

March 11th · 11:52 AM

The Front Row Scholar

There is something almost unbearably intimate about encountering a person you love inside the architecture of their ordinary life — not in the broad campus thoroughfares where the encounter is brief and public and over before it has properly begun, but in the contained, quiet interior of a shared space where the encounter has duration, and you can study without the usual urgency. NC312. You sat in the absolute front row, in that seat which announces a particular relationship with the world: not ambition, exactly, but a complete willingness to be fully present to what is being offered, an unwillingness to allow the back rows and their comfortable distance to absorb the attention before it reaches its intended destination.

You were bent forward slightly over your notes, your pen moving in the steady, unhurried rhythm of a person writing not to impress but to genuinely capture — to prevent something valuable from escaping. The late morning light fell across your shoulder from the classroom window, and in it I could see the particular concentration that your face achieves when you are genuinely engaged: a slight softening of the brow, a stillness of the features around the eyes that speaks of an interior process quietly proceeding. You were utterly, completely, gorgeously absorbed in the lecture. You were the exact image that the word scholar makes in the mind of someone who loves learning for its own sake — not the performative version, not the anxious, grade-driven version, but the pure version: a person sitting in a room paying attention because paying attention is itself a pleasure.

I sat at the extreme periphery of that lecture hall and tried, with the limited success one might expect, to also pay attention to the lecture. The professor spoke. I heard syllables. The room was full of the usual rustling and note-taking and muffled phone-checking and shifting in seats. And at the front of it all, the still centre around which all this peripheral activity orbited, you simply were. A quiet scholar in a sunlit row, writing things down, carrying the unhurried gravity of a person entirely at home in her own mind. I have thought about that image many times since. It returns with the reliability of a favourite song.

March 12th · 8:55 PM

A Slapstick Tragedy

I want to be honest about this evening: it was, by any objective measure, an embarrassment. And I want to include it here, in this ledger that I have tried to keep honest, because the truth of what I am documenting is not only composed of the beautiful moments — the luminous sightings, the quiet epiphanies, the six-minute eternities and the evenings of Tulsi tea. It is also composed of the moments in which the silent devotion reveals its more absurd face, in which the religion of attention produces, instead of transcendence, a pratfall.

You were near the SNIG gate that evening, at precisely the point where the RK-SNIG road terminates and surrenders its concrete authority to the footpath — and you were deep in conversation with a boy who sat astride a bicycle with the easy, proprietary posture of someone entirely comfortable with the geography of another person's attention. He was talking; you were listening with that characteristically full-body engagement I have catalogued in you, your whole bearing oriented toward him in the slightly-forward lean of genuine interest. And I felt — and I want to be clear that I knew this was irrational, that I was tracking this irrationality in real time and it made no difference whatsoever — a sharp, furious, entirely unjustifiable pang of envy. Not at his appearance or his social standing or any quality of him in particular. At the sheer, effortless, unstudied ease with which he occupied the space adjacent to you. The way his existence simply permitted him to be where he was, as though it were nothing. As though it were the most ordinary thing in the world.

I was walking with Arnav. Arnav was saying something. I know this because his mouth was moving in my peripheral vision and sounds were emerging from it that had the grammatical shape of language. My mind had, without consulting me, entirely vacated the vicinity of whatever he was saying and relocated itself to the SNIG gate, where it remained, fully occupied, while my body continued to propel itself along the footpath with the reliable momentum of a person who has forgotten that locomotion requires more than forward motion — it requires also a modicum of spatial awareness. I walked directly into Arnav. Not a brush, not a glancing contact, but a full-bodied collision of the kind that requires a subsequent renegotiation of where both parties are in space. We stumbled. Arnav expressed, in the vocabulary of mild, bewildered profanity, his opinion of the situation. I recovered my balance and offered an explanation that was, I think, insufficiently convincing. Somewhere along the SNIG gate footpath, a boy on a bicycle continued to talk, and you continued to listen, and the universe found the whole scene deeply amusing.

The Terrifying Spark

I have been trying, for some weeks now, to find language adequate to what happened outside the TP class on this Tuesday morning in March, and I am beginning to suspect that the experience itself is resistant to language — that it belongs to the category of things that can be circled and approached from many angles without ever being precisely named. Let me try anyway, because trying is all I have.

You were dressed that morning in a short, bluish-dotted kurti paired with black lowers and your faithful Crocs — an outfit that had a lightness to it, a spring-adjacency, as though your wardrobe had registered the calendar's progression before the weather had fully committed. You were approaching from across the space outside the TP class, and I was in my usual posture of elaborate casualness, which is to say the posture of someone who is working very hard at appearing to not be doing the thing they are doing.

And then it happened. Our eyes met. This alone was not unusual — our eyes had met before, in August, in the preliminary encounter that inaugurated everything, and it had been extraordinary in the way first encounters always are: overwhelming, but temporary, quickly subsumed by the forward march of ordinary time. What happened on March 17th was different in its quality. Different in its texture and its weight. Because in this meeting of eyes I felt, with a certainty I have never felt about anything before or since, that something registered. Not recognition in the full sense — you did not know my name, or my story, or the nature of what I have been carrying since August. But recognition in a narrower, more terrifying sense: your eyes located me. They did not slide past me the way eyes slide past furniture or background architecture. They stopped. For a fraction of a second that lasted much longer than its official duration, you looked at me in the particular way that one looks at a thing one has seen before but cannot quite place — the slight narrowing, the almost-question, the beginning of a file being opened in the mind's archive.

I felt a jolt down my spine so specific and complete that I cannot describe it as anything other than electrical. The invisible string I had been holding for seven months pulled suddenly, violently taut, and for a fraction of a second we were connected by it — not romantically, not transactionally, but in the way two nodes in a circuit are connected: a current passed, briefly and without announcement, between us. You looked away. I stopped breathing until you did. And then you were gone around the corner, and I was standing outside the TP class with the residual current still humming in my chest, aware that something had irrevocably changed in the cartography of this thing between us, even if the new cartography was not, and would never be, a map with two people on it.

The Archaeology of Memory

Near the foyer of NC312, between the first and second floor landing, where the staircase makes its brief pause before continuing upward — a liminal space, threshold architecture, the kind of place where encounters happen that are too brief to be events but too weighted to be forgotten. You wore loose blue jeans, Crocs, and a top — and the top is the reason I am writing this entry with such care, because the top was not merely a garment on this occasion. It was a temporal event.

I had, in the quiet hours of late-night research that I will not defend but also will not pretend did not happen, encountered your 12th-class photograph. In it you wore this top — this specific, half-length, particular top — and I had spent some time with that photograph, as one spends time with a document one is studying, noting the ways the younger version of you was already entirely you, the continuities of expression and bearing and the particular quality of your repose that suggest a self already fully formed, merely waiting for the world to catch up. I had filed the top in the archive. I had not expected to see the archive come to life.

But there it was. The same top, now animated, now ascending the stairs of NC312, now belonging to the living, breathing, present-tense version of you rather than the preserved, past-tense version I had encountered on a screen in the small hours of the morning. The effect was extraordinary and entirely disproportionate to the event. I felt like an archaeologist who has spent months carefully excavating a site and who suddenly, unexpectedly, encounters the civilisation itself — not its artefacts but its living members, going about their lives in the full, unembarrassed sunshine, entirely unaware that someone has been brushing the dust off their remnants one careful centimetre at a time. The past and the present collided in the foyer of NC312, and I was the only witness, and the collision left me standing very still on a staircase landing while you continued upward, oblivious, into your future.

The Night Wanderer

There are evenings that fracture — that refuse to cohere into a single narrative, that break apart into disconnected scenes the way a film breaks apart when the projector stutters. I watched your group begin the long walk toward MS. I watched until the darkness reclaimed you. I was. It cost me: a U-turn, a confused and reluctant Arnav extracted from his room under some pretended pretext, a manufactured route that took us back along the road your group had taken. And as we finally walked past you — a feat of geography assembled from equal parts desire and absurdity — your eyes were fixed downward. You were not looking at anything except the private landscape of your own thoughts, entirely absorbed in whatever interior country you were travelling through at that late hour. And I walked past you in the campus night, having expended considerable effort for a non-encounter, and I found — here is the strange part — that I did not feel foolish. The Phantom Chaser

Morning was unremarkable in its beauty — you at the subway gate with those two familiar friends, dressed in shirt and jeans and Crocs, existing with the easy consistency of a person who is comfortable with the rhythms of her own life. I filed it. I carried it through the afternoon.

But the night was a different creature entirely. It was the night I chased a ghost of you through the dark streets of the campus and spent twenty minutes living inside a case of mistaken identity so complete and so self-generated that it deserves its own entry in any future study of the human capacity for wishful perception.

You had been walking from the LBS-SNVH road at 9:54 PM — this I know for certain because I saw you, briefly, before you pulled toward the Pepsi cut and then vanished in the vicinity of the Gymkhana with an abruptness that left me standing on a footpath with no object for my attention. I spent twenty minutes wandering the relevant geography in a state of low-grade, persistent searching. And then I found you — or believed I had found you. A silhouette ahead of me at a distance, moving from MT in the direction of the Pepsi cut, with a gait that in the dark seemed to carry the rhythm I knew. I followed. I followed with the quiet, steady conviction of someone who knows they are right, who does not need to close the distance because the confirmation of sight from this distance is sufficient. I followed for several minutes. And then a streetlight intervened, with the blunt honesty of a fact, and illuminated the face of an entirely different person who had never heard of me and had no interest in the situation at all.

The defeat was physical. It settled in my chest the way cold water settles in a room — pervasive, lowering the temperature of everything. I stood for a moment with this stranger's face still registering in my eyes, processing the collapse of the last twenty minutes. And then fate, which occasionally shows mercy, arranged the world one more time: as I turned back, you appeared. The real you. Walking peacefully with your group of four, unaware that a search party had been looking for you, unaware that your shadow had been chased through the campus dark by a fool with a compass needle where his heart ought to be. The relief was a physical sensation — a literal release of held breath, a lightening of the chest, the specific, animal comfort of a lung filling with air after too long without it. I went home that night with the embarrassing certainty that I would do it all again.

The Comfort of Rhythm

The subway gate again. The exact same attire as the morning before. Your friend beside you. The same unhurried gait, the same self-possessed bearing, the same quality of presence that organises the space around it without announcing itself or seeking acknowledgment. It was, by every external measure, identical to a dozen other mornings I have catalogued in this ledger. And yet.

There is a concept in music called the ground bass — a repeating harmonic figure in the lowest register over which the upper voices are free to ornament, to improvise, to develop and vary. The ground bass does not change. It is the thing that makes the variations possible, the stable foundation against which difference becomes audible. You are my ground bass. The predictability of you — your consistent aesthetic, your rhythmic presence at particular locations at particular hours, the way your existence follows patterns I have learned to read the way one learns to read the weather — is not tedium. It is the opposite of tedium. It is the very thing that makes each variation meaningful. The white t-shirt against the maroon hoodie. The morning laughter against the nighttime silence. The front-row scholar against the evening wanderer. Each difference is only legible because the ground bass continues, unwavering, beneath it.

I saw you for thirty seconds that morning. You disappeared into the campus machinery of the day. I carried the thirty seconds for the rest of it, like a stone in a pocket — smooth-edged, comforting to the touch, a physical reminder that the world contains within it this particular, irreplaceable thing.

The Descent into Despair

There comes, in the lifecycle of every private devotion, a moment that the devotion has not prepared you for — a moment in which the beautiful, sustaining fiction of distance collapses, and what it reveals underneath is not the floor you expected but a drop you did not.

Mid-April. 9:30 PM. I think I am so done with her now, I said to the dark, and the dark, with characteristic indifference, said nothing back.

I saw you at TOAT with two men who were, in the blunt vocabulary of the campus, what people mean when they say physically imposing — broad-shouldered, capable, carrying in their bodies the easy confidence of people who have never had reason to doubt that they belong where they are. You were sitting between them in grey pyjamas and a black t-shirt and your perpetual Crocs, and you were engaged in the conversation with the kind of whole-body investment I know from you — leaning slightly forward, animated, your hands moving occasionally as you made some point about a song playing at high volume. You looked the way you always look: specific, particular, irreducibly yourself. But you were looking at them.

I was seated two seats to your right. The mathematics of this proximity were, that evening, nothing short of cruel. I could hear the cadence of your voice — not the words, the music beneath the words, the particular rise and fall of a specific human voice that I had observed only from distances too great to permit this precision. I could, if I am honest, detect the faintest suggestion of your perfume. And you were, across this impossible nearness, in a world I could not enter. Not because you had locked me out — you had not, and would not, because locking something out requires first noticing it — but because the world you occupied had its own internal logic, its own gravitational field, its own complete and self-sufficient atmosphere, and I was outside all of it, as I have always been, as I will always be.

Arnav's report came later. He had seen you outside Mio Amore at 8:00 PM, he said — seated in a chair, two feet from one of those same men, engaged in conversation that had the intensity and the focus of something that mattered to both parties. And the panopticon of my own careful, sustained, devoted construction — the beautiful edifice I had built over seven months of patient, loving attention — revealed itself, in that moment, for what it had perhaps always been: a room with no door. A prison that faces inward. A tower I had built around myself and mistaken for a vantage point. The beautiful melancholy I had spent months attributing to you had, without my noticing, been quietly relocating. It had packed its things and moved, without leaving a forwarding address, into the hollow centre of my own chest.

I have never, in twenty-odd years of existence, felt more comprehensively alone.

A Hesitant Resurrection

April 13th. 6:24 PM. I emerged from Main Building into the long, low light of a late afternoon in the second week of April, and the light was doing something extraordinary — the specific, golden, horizontal light of a sun approaching the horizon, which lends to the ordinary surfaces of buildings and trees and footpaths a quality they do not possess at other hours, a warmth that is also a kind of grief because it is so unambiguously temporary. The campus was full of people moving through this light, each of them carrying their own story through the amber air. And something in me — some small, persistent, unkillable thing that I had assumed died in the seat two places to your right at TOAT — stirred. Not joyfully. Not with confidence. With the tentative, uncertain quality of something that has been hurt and is testing, very carefully, whether the ground beneath it will hold. A hesitant resurrection, this: more question than answer, more opening than arrival. Whether it constitutes hope, I cannot say. Whether hope, in this particular situation, is wisdom or folly, I also cannot say. What I can say is that the light on the April campus was extraordinarily beautiful that evening, and that I was awake to it, which is perhaps — perhaps — enough.
